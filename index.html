About Me

I've loved astronomy since the second grade when I read a book on Saturn. This promptly led me to seek out a book on Mercury because books on the Solar System should be read in the proper order. At least that's how 7 year old Jessica felt. And after finishing the book on Pluto (because Pluto was still a planet at the time), I immediately began reading about stars and galaxies and anything else related to astronomy. While this story may seem like a cliche, it still remains an important part of my life. At that moment, I decided I was going to be an astronomer and nothing over the next few decades would change my mind. In fact, the more answers I find, the more questions I have, and the more I love it every day.

Outside of astronomy research, I enjoy running and going to the gym, as well as cross-stitching and sewing quilts. Stay tuned for updates on current crafty projects!

Research

My main research area is the study of planets around other stars, also known as exoplanets. In particular, I work on understanding their atmospheres by learning what they are composed of using a method called transmission spectroscopy. While every exoplanet is pretty neat, my main interest lies in solving the mystery of the existance of low-density planets called "super-puffs." Think of a super-puff as a planet the size of Jupiter, but only 1/100th its mass (density of cotton candy). Super weird right? This interest started while studying three super-puffs called Kepler-51 b,c,d (astronomers are great at naming planets) with the goal of figuring out what their atmospheres were made of. Instead, we discovered that these planets were covered with a haze or cloud layer at the top of their atmosphere, leading to more questions and less answers (as always). This might actually be a common feature of super-puffs, but why this is, well we don't know. I'm also curious about how these planets formed, how their atmospheres evolve over time (do they blow away or keep it), and really, why are they so puffy?!

I also study the atmospheres around higher density exoplanets called super-Earths. Actually, I am working on first finding an atmosphere around these types of planets before I can study it. Since the atmospheres of these planets are so thin, we need high precision to detect any molecular features and find an Earth-like atmosphere. The future James Webb Space Telescope should be able to detect these atmospheres, but until then, I'll keep trying with the telescopes we have and prepare for the future!

Of course, in order to study these types of exoplanets, we first need to find them! Using TESS, Kepler, and ground-based observatories like the Apache Point Observatory, I search for and help confirm new exoplanets by looking for small dips in star light as the planet passes in front of its star (called a transit).
